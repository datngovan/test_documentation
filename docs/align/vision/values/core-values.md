# Core Values

> **Owner:** People Operations | **Last Updated:** 2026-01-10

## Our Five Core Values

These are not aspirations — they are the behaviors we hire for, promote for, and part ways over.

---

### 1. Ship with Integrity

We do what we say we'll do, on the timeline we committed to. When we can't, we say so early and clearly — not after the deadline passes. Code we ship is code we stand behind: tested, documented, and ready to maintain.

**In practice:**
- If a deadline is at risk, raise it 3+ days early — never the day before
- Never merge code you wouldn't want to own in production at 2 AM
- Estimates are commitments, not wishes

---

### 2. Default to Transparency

We share information freely. Bad news travels faster than good news internally. Mistakes are discussed openly, not buried. Strategy is shared with everyone — not just leadership.

**In practice:**
- Document decisions in writing, not just in meetings
- When something goes wrong, write the post-mortem before moving on
- Share quarterly financials and OKR progress with the whole company

---

### 3. Obsess Over the Customer

Every product decision starts with a customer problem. We don't build features because they're technically interesting or because a competitor has them — we build them because a customer's work is better for it.

**In practice:**
- Before writing a line of code, write the user story from the customer's perspective
- Talk to at least one customer per sprint (engineers included)
- Track adoption, not just delivery — a feature no one uses didn't ship

---

### 4. Raise the Bar

We hold ourselves and each other to high standards. A good pull request has tests. Good documentation exists. Good meetings have agendas. We improve the codebase we touch — we don't just pass problems downstream.

**In practice:**
- Code reviews are about the code, not the person — be specific, not vague
- Leave every system slightly better than you found it (Boy Scout Rule)
- Giving honest feedback is an act of respect, not aggression

---

### 5. Move with Urgency

Speed is a competitive advantage. A good decision made now beats a perfect decision made next month. We prefer reversible fast actions over irreversible slow ones — and we bias toward action when the cost of inaction is high.

**In practice:**
- Two-day rule: if a decision can be made in two days, make it in two days
- Ship something to users before it feels completely ready
- Disagree and commit — once a decision is made, execute fully

---

## Values in Tension

Values sometimes pull in opposite directions. Here's how we navigate the most common conflicts:

| Tension | Resolution |
|---------|-----------|
| Ship with Integrity vs. Move with Urgency | Urgency wins for reversible decisions; Integrity wins for security, data, and customer-facing promises |
| Transparency vs. Customer Confidentiality | Confidential customer data is never transparency — protect it absolutely |
| Raise the Bar vs. Move with Urgency | When shipping: good enough now beats perfect later. When maintaining: raise the bar during refactoring sprints, not in the middle of a feature |
